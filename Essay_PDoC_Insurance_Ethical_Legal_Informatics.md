# Reforming Insurance Coverage for Prolonged Disorders of Consciousness: Analyzing Ethical, Legal, and Health Informatics Systems to Inform an Equitable Long-Term Care Framework

**Author:** Diana Cortes  
**Written:** May 2025  
__Revised *:__ January 2026



## Introduction
Prolonged disorders of consciousness (PDoC) are amongst the most acute outcomes of severe acquired brain injuries (sABIs), affecting around 1,650 individuals annually (Wade, 2018). Still, the path to understanding and 
providing appropriate long-term care (LTC) for these patients remains filled with medical, technological, legal, and ethical complexities. As a consequence, patients with PDoC frequently face restricted access to necessary 
care and resources (Brown & Finkelstein, 2011; Zhang et al., 2023). This paper argues that PDoC is currently being managed within a self-perpetuating insurance and legal system that falls short of its ethical obligations, 
and that breaking this cycle requires a coordinated restructuring of LTC coverage through enforceable legal protections, ethical oversight, and leveraged health information technology (HIT).

Unlike transient or less severe disorders of consciousness (DoC), PDoC patients are more likely to require sustained, complex LTC, making them especially vulnerable to the consequences of the current system. 
Therefore, it is necessary to contextualize where PDoC lies within the broader DoC spectrum. Consciousness is clinically defined by two key components: wakefulness and awareness. Clinical wakefulness refers to reflexive or 
involuntary responsiveness to stimuli. Awareness involves the ability to process sensory information and respond intentionally. An impairment in the spectra of wakefulness and awareness following sABI results in a disorder 
of consciousness (Gosseries et al., 2011). Patients are considered to have PDoC when they remain in a reduced consciousness state for more than 28 days (Giacino et al., 2018a). PDoC patients are further categorized as being 
in a persistent vegetative state (PVS) or minimally conscious state (MCS), based on the severity of their impairments (Gosseries et al., 2011). Patients may fluctuate between these substates, exhibiting behaviors that range 
from reflexive responses to intermittent intentional movements. However, since these behaviors are inconsistent and lack functional applicability, PDoC patients remain fully dependent on others for all aspects of care.

The ethical principles of autonomy, best-interest, beneficence, nonmaleficence, and justice are essential for guiding LTC decisions in PDoC cases. _Figure 1_ outlines these core principles and their fundamental characteristics 
that shape ethically sound care practices. Unfortunately, these principles are frequently jeopardized when evaluating the current system's approach to PDoC care. Autonomy and best-interest principles are often undermined when 
insurance companies restrict access to LTC based on a limited prognosis. Beneficence and nonmaleficence are compromised when insurance decisions prioritize cost-effectiveness over adequate care, potentially resulting in harm. 
Lastly, justice is not upheld when insurance policies disproportionately limit care based on the uncertain nature of PDoC recovery, leading to inequitable access to needed services.

## Ethical Incongruences and Health Informatics Limitations in the Current System

Despite existing legal frameworks and ethical commitments to nondiscriminatory access to LTC, systemic gaps persist, allowing insurers to deny adequate support to PDoC patients. As illustrated in _Figure 2_, current legal 
frameworks theoretically align with core ethical principles and appear to fulfill the needs of patients with PDoC. In reality, patients are routinely denied vital rehabilitative services based on narrowly defined criteria 
that prioritize recovery over sustained quality-of-life (QoL) care (Zhang et al., 2023). The regulatory ambiguity regarding LTC and practically nonexistent PDoC-specific protections enable insurers to exploit loopholes and 
legally justify withholding coverage. The discrepancy between what is promised by law and what is delivered in practice signifies more than a policy flaw. It reveals the bitter irony that the very system designed to protect 
and provide for these patients becomes a source of harm that facilitates their abandonment.

Insurance practices often produce ethically unjust outcomes by favoring rapid recovery trajectories, overlooking the complex, individualized nature of healing from sABIs. PDoC patients require a wide range of rehabilitative 
and maintenance services over extended periods since only about 30% of them regain consciousness, and still, most will continue living with varying degrees of long-term disability. Additionally, their average survival rate 
ranges from 1 to 5 years, with some patients living more than a decade post-injury (Yan et al., 2024). Conventional health insurance does not cover LTC for chronic illness, and while Medicaid and private LTC insurance are 
intended to fill that void, they can also fail to deliver the necessary coverage (Brown & Finkelstein, 2011). However, research suggests that significant but subtle changes can still occur in these phases, even if they cannot be 
immediately measured through standard diagnostic tools (Giacino et al., 2018a). Moreover, newer findings challenge the outdated 1-year prognostic timeline for potential recovery, as there are now well-documented cases of recovery 
10 or more years after injury (Pavlov et al., 2024; Yan et al., 2024). In many cases, insurers may treat the plateau phase as a finality or continue to use the disproved 1-year marker as a rationale to reduce or deny continued 
therapy (Zhang et al., 2023). _Figure 3_ depicts a condensed version of a patient's journey, whose recovery slows down after initial rehabilitation. When rehabilitation and stimulation are cut off, the brain stops receiving the 
vital input it needs to heal and begins to atrophy (Edlow et al., 2021). In the end, the unjust outcomes of these practices go beyond unmet needs. They also entail a reduced likelihood of minimal but meaningful recovery and 
preventable medical decline when services are cut off prematurely.

As insurance decisions are guided by data filtered through HIT systems, these systems hold the power to either advance equitable coverage or reinforce the barriers that lead to coverage denial. Further innovations in medical 
technology are still needed to care for this population, especially around diagnostic tools, as none of the latest imaging technology has shown moderate or strong evidence to provide an accurate diagnosis 
(Giacino et al., 2018a; Giacino et al., 2018b). However, while better imaging tools are being developed, other current HITs already have the potential to support evidence-based, ethical coverage decisions. _Figure 4_ exemplifies 
how some core HIT components can directly contribute to improving continuity of care and decision-making, or exacerbate gaps and misuse of information (Giacino et al., 2018b; Kruse et al., 2017). Nevertheless, what stands in the 
way of any ideal use of HITs is a fundamental lack of standardization. Without unified population-specific protocols for collecting, interpreting, and exchanging data across settings, no matter how much technology advances, 
the output will continue to be inconsistent, and the system will remain inefficient. Moreover, even if every single PDoC patient were to get accurately diagnosed and had properly coordinated care, the inherently slow and variable 
process of neurological recovery would still require an extensive amount of time, patience, and ongoing support.

PDoC patients are surviving at the mercy of legal and healthcare systems that formally comply with ethical standards, but fail to ensure meaningful care in practice. Laws such as the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) and 
Section 504 protect against discrimination, and the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) policies are designed to promote access to LTC. Clinically, continuous stimulation is recognized as essential for brain healing, and 
today’s HITs are reasonably capable of supporting optimal care. Yet, even with these protections and clinical knowledge in place, PDoC patients are still effectively denied services, their brains are deprived of critical stimulation, 
and the potential of available HIT remains underutilized. Therefore, the question is: why are PDoC patients still routinely granted access to only — or even less than — the bare minimum care, despite what we know is necessary and 
possible to provide? The answer points to a troubling, self-perpetuating cycle in which service denials lead to deterioration, and the deterioration is then used to justify further denials.


## A Systemic Snowball Effect

This self-perpetuating cycle is not a simple closed loop but a compound of systemic failures that grows more ethically fraught at every stage, creating a seemingly inescapable snowball effect. As care becomes increasingly 
inaccessible after patients reach a plateau phase, medical decline follows suit, and their QoL becomes so diminished that family members may consider euthanasia or treatment withdrawal. However, many find these avenues obstructed by 
legal ambivalence or procedural red tape, all in the name of the best-interest principle. This systemic trap practically implies that it is in the patient's best interest to remain in this prolonged state of suffering and all but 
refuses to apply the same best-interest principle to justify access to care that could alleviate harm or prevent it in the first place. This impasse leaves a population with growing needs and diminishing options that further buries the 
already-stressed caregivers and entire healthcare system. Despite this dire demand, innovation and standardization initiatives remain deprioritized for this population. Perhaps a gruesome theory for this might be that, in a system driven 
by outcomes and efficiency, due to their limited prognosis and inability to contribute economically, PDoC patients may be categorized as a "not worthwhile investment". Yet their lives still warrant attention, dignity, and compassion because, 
regardless of their uncertain contribution to society, their suffering is undeniably real, and their right to nondiscrimination remains unchanged.

The resulting crisis is not inescapable since what has been created can also be reimagined. To truly honor the ethical principles often cited but rarely upheld, it is necessary to pursue targeted reforms that can reshape insurance coverage 
for the LTC needs of patients with PDoC.


## Breaking the Cycle by Reforming the System

In order to break the snowball effect, reform solutions must not only address insurance policies and health information practices but also restore the ethical foundation that has been lost. _Figure 6_ proposes a stacked-solutions approach 
where each level supports and strengthens the next. _Figure 6_ also illustrates that the proposed solutions could be cohesively integrated into the existing legal structures as sections of the Affordable Care Act (ACA) and/or as CMS conditions 
of participation.

At the foundation of the restructuring process is the need for transparency and ethical oversight. Implementing insurance ethics review boards would add a formal check to warn against systemic biases and routine patterns of coverage denials, 
particularly those that are disparately directed at patients with an unfavorable prognosis for functional outcomes. Simultaneously mandating public disclosure in coverage decision-making for approvals and denials would enhance accountability 
and enable data collection on the long-term effects of covered care. This foundation ensures that the new system will be particularly grounded in the values of justice and nonmaleficence.  

The second level focuses on a healthcare system infrastructure that delivers quality care and data integrity. As previously noted, standardizing collection, interpretation, and data exchange is necessary to ensure that complete and accurate 
records inform medical decision-making. To ensure these data integrity and interoperability standards are upheld, it is necessary to introduce accreditation requirements specific to facilities treating PDoC patients. The accreditation 
requirements would also press for care competencies aligned with clinical guidelines and ethical practices across the multiple settings responsible for the continuum of PDoC care. These standards and accreditations cultivate beneficence, 
ensuring that PDoC patients receive the highest level of care through a unified evidence-based approach to holistic well-being.

From there, the quality data obtained should drive the refinement of predictive analytics to reduce systemic bias and improve comprehensive decision-making. PDoC-specific medical models must evolve in parallel with insurance claims review 
models, incorporating stratification techniques for a more equitable approach to rehabilitation and maintenance care. This way, both LTC medical and insurance plans can be designed with a medically-informed best-interest principle at their core. 

The top of the pyramid holds one of the most critical reforms: ensuring and protecting LTC coverage for PDoC patients while penalizing arbitrary insurance denials. Reinforced PDoC-specific legal protections must prevent insurers from 
prematurely cutting off rehabilitative coverage based on an uncertain prognosis or cost-efficiency alone. Legal regulations must ensure that PDoC patients with evidence-based predictions of low chances of recovery have guaranteed access to 
high-quality maintenance care services that prioritize pain management and QoL. Lastly, insurance companies should be held accountable by penalizing unjust or unlawful coverage denials. By punishing these actions, the system would revalidate 
the responsibility of offering ongoing, patient-focused care to make certain that PDoC patients are not treated as low-economic-value throwaways. This third action revalidates the values of best-interest, beneficence, nonmaleficence, and 
justice and strengthens the ability to restore the autonomy of PDoC patients. 

## Conclusion

There must be a comprehensive restructuring of the PDoC insurance coverage and care-delivery system in order to reclaim respect and dignity for this often-overlooked population. Supported by reforms favoring healthcare informatics 
standardization and stronger legal frameworks that promote accountability, the system could finally meet the crucial need for equitable and ethical care. That way, insurance companies would be legally obligated to provide actual long-term 
coverage that prioritizes quality of life over cost-cutting measures. Ultimately, what these patients need is a supportive infrastructure that allows them to take time to heal. Most importantly, what these human beings deserve is a healthcare 
system that does not abandon them if they cannot recover quickly or at all. These reforms would protect the rights of PDoC patients and ensure that the U.S. healthcare system aligns with ethical responsibilities, guaranteeing that each patient 
receives the level of compassionate care they would hope for themselves or a loved one in a similar situation.
