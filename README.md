# About This Repo

## Overview 
This repository contains a finalized academic essay developed as a final project for a graduate-level course in _Ethical and Legal Issues in Healthcare Informatics_.

__Project Title:__ _Reforming Insurance Coverage for Prolonged Disorders of Consciousness: Analyzing Ethical, Legal, and Health Informatics Systems to Inform an Equitable Long-Term Care Framework_

The essay presents a constructive, ethics-driven analysis of current care systems for patients with prolonged disorders of consciousness (PDoC) and concludes with a multilevel reform proposal. While insurance coverage serves as the primary decision point shaping access to long-term care (LTC), the analysis demonstrates how insurance practices are structurally influenced by legal frameworks, ethical oversight mechanisms, and health information technology (HIT). The proposed reforms emerge at the intersection of patient needs, adaptations to existing legal structures, and strategic improvements in HIT that collectively inform insurance decision-making. 

> Note: The project was completed under strict word count constraints. Custom-designed graphics were used to convey complex information efficiently while reducing text length.

### Use of Graphics & Visualization
A key focus of this project is the use of custom-designed graphics to:

### Purpose & Goals
  
#### Purpose
I spent three years working with patients with disorders of consciousness (DoC) and PDoC in a Long-Term Acute Care inpatient setting, where I developed a deep professional commitment to this population. During this time, I observed persistent gaps and ethical challenges in the U.S. long-term care system, many of which were driven or reinforced by insurance coverage determinations, but lacked the tools to analyze them in depth.

This project provided an opportunity to move beyond experiential knowledge and to advocate for these patients using evidence-based analysis and insights gained through my master’s coursework. I chose to focus specifically on PDoC because individuals in this group are more likely to require highly complex, lifelong medical services and may therefore be among the most significantly affected subgroups within the broader DoC spectrum. 

Ultimately, this essay aims to raise awareness of the lived experiences, systemic challenges, and inherent value of both patients and their caregivers within the healthcare system, while encouraging more ethically grounded and systems-aware approaches to care and coverage. The resulting proposed ethically-driven framework is intended as a conceptual tool to support discussion, education, and future policy or informatics development.

#### Goals
The primary intent of this work is utilize selected ethical principles as a compass to: 
- Identify inconsistencies and gaps in current care systems.
- Acknowledge the inherent complexities of long-term care for PDoC patients.
- Highlight how systemic issues and practices self-perpetuate inequities.
- Propose high-level reforms across the system to improve patient care and support.

### Scope

The project's analysis is grounded in the ethical principles of autonomy, best-interest, beneficence, nonmaleficence, and justice, and draws on relevant research, legal standards, and healthcare system conditions as they existed in Spring 2025. These principles are used to examine legal frameworks, insurance coverage policies, care practices, and health information technology challenges affecting individuals with PDoC. While PDoC serves as the primary focus, many of the ethical and systemic issues discussed are applicable to DoC more broadly. 

Inequities in PDoC care are presented not solely as the product of individual policies, but also as the result of misaligned systems that collectively shape insurance behavior. The essay explores the role of legal interpretation, ethical reasoning, health information outputs, and prognostic data in insurance coverage decisions to grant, restrict, or deny access for patients with PDoC. This analysis, therefore, frames insurance reform as a systems-level challenge rather than an isolated policy issue.

The primary focus of the essay is to examine and synthesize existing systemic issue, the project intentionally moves beyond critique and includes proposed solution. However, it is presented at a high-level framework solution rather than a fully developed policy or technical implementation. The framework is introduced to illustrate how ethical and legal principles _could_ be operationalized within healthcare systems, particularly through the application of health informatics and clinical decision-support concepts.

### Strengths & Limitations

#### Strengths
- __Clinically grounded perspective__
  - My clinical background of working directly with this population for three years provided firsthand insight into care realities, patient trajectories, and systemic gaps that are often underrepresented in policy-focused discussions.
    
- __Integrated ethical analysis__
  - The project utilizes ethical principles, legal considerations, insurance practices, and health information technology concepts to examine PDoC care from a systems-level perspective. This approach strengthens the analysis by situating patient experiences within broader structural and policy contexts.
    
- __Strong visual communication and systems thinking__
  - A central strength of this project lies in its use of visual models and diagrams to convey complex ethical, legal, and systemic relationships. These graphics were thoughtfully designed to communicate layered concepts efficiently and have been validated as effective instructional examples in subsequent course offerings.
    
- __Clear argumentative structure__
  - Despite strict word-count constraints, the essay presents a coherent and well-supported argument that progresses logically from problem identification to ethical analysis and high-level solution framing.


#### Limitations
- __Generalized concepts__
  - Due to word count constraints, insurance coverage practices, and patient experiences are highly generalized. This underrepresents real-world variability across insurers, jurisdictions, and individual patient circumstances, which may affect how the proposed arguments translate across settings.

- __Scope and level of technical specificity__
  - As an argument-driven analysis, the proposed solution frameworks are intentionally high-level and conceptual. They do not attempt to dive into the full operational, regulatory, or financial complexity of implementing reforms within highly interconnected healthcare systems. 

- __Evolving expertise in health informatics and legal domains__
  - At the time of writing, I was transitioning from a primarily clinical role into formal training in health informatics and healthcare law. As a result, certain technical, regulatory, or legal implementation considerations are necessarily simplified or abstracted to support ethical and policy-level discussion.

- __Perspective shaped by proximity to patient care__
  - The analysis is influenced by close professional engagement with this patient population. While this perspective strengthens the ethical grounding of the work, it may also emphasize patient-centered priorities over institutional or administrative constraints.
